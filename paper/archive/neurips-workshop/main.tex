\begin{abstract}
Ternary quantization (1.58-bit weights) incurs 3--9\% accuracy gaps versus FP32 ResNets on CIFAR. We find that data augmentation \emph{widens} this gap: FP32 models benefit 1.5--3.7$\times$ more than ternary models. Through layer-wise ablation, we identify the first convolutional layer as responsible for 54--74\% of the loss. Combining a simple fix (keeping conv1 in FP32, adding 0.08\% parameters) with knowledge distillation recovers 89\% of the gap on CIFAR-10 and \emph{exceeds FP32} on harder tasks: +1.0\% on CIFAR-100 ($p=0.028$), +1.7\% on Tiny ImageNet ($p=0.004$). Testing across four CNN families reveals the recipe works well for standard convolutions (80--130\% recovery for ResNet) but poorly for depthwise separable designs (17--73\% for MobileNetV2).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ternary quantization ($\{-1,0,+1\}$ weights) promises $20\times$ compression and $64\times$ theoretical speedup through integer-only arithmetic. For CNNs, we find stubborn accuracy gaps: 3.5--9.1\% on CIFAR for ResNets, 8--14\% for MobileNetV2/EfficientNet. A natural hypothesis is that data augmentation---a standard technique for improving generalization---might close this gap. Ternary networks, with limited capacity, could benefit disproportionately from stronger regularization.

\textbf{Surprisingly, it widens the gap.} Moving from basic to aggressive augmentation increases the deficit in every configuration by up to 59\%, with FP32 benefiting 1.5--3.7$\times$ more. This augmentation asymmetry reveals ternary networks lack the capacity to exploit additional training signal from stronger augmentation.

Layer-wise ablation shows the first convolutional layer (\texttt{conv1}) accounts for 54--74\% of the gap despite only 0.08\% of parameters. Combining this insight with knowledge distillation (KD) from an FP32 teacher yields an effective recipe: on CIFAR-10, we recover 89\% of the gap; on harder tasks (CIFAR-100, Tiny ImageNet), we \emph{exceed FP32 accuracy} by +1.0\% and +1.7\% respectively (Figure~\ref{fig:recipe}). Testing across four CNN families reveals strong architecture dependence: 80--130\% recovery for ResNet, but only 17--73\% for MobileNetV2.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Binary and Ternary CNNs.} BinaryConnect~\cite{courbariaux2015binaryconnect} and XNOR-Net~\cite{rastegari2016xnornet} pioneered binary quantization using the straight-through estimator. Ternary Weight Networks (TWN)~\cite{li2016ternary} demonstrated that adding zero improves over strict binary. Trained Ternary Quantization (TTQ)~\cite{zhu2017ttq} achieved near-FP32 accuracy by learning asymmetric scaling factors per layer, at the cost of requiring floating-point multiply during inference. \bitnetb{}~\cite{ma2024bitnetb158} uses fixed $\{-1,0,+1\}$ weights enabling pure integer arithmetic.

\textbf{Knowledge Distillation.} Hinton et al.~\cite{hinton2015distilling} introduced KD via soft labels. QKD~\cite{kim2019qkd} showed distillation helps binary networks. We use standard soft-label distillation ($T=4$, $\alpha=0.9$) without task-specific tuning.

\textbf{Mixed-Precision.} Keeping first and last layers at higher precision is standard practice~\cite{rastegari2016xnornet,zhou2016dorefa}. HAQ~\cite{wang2019haq} and HAWQ~\cite{dong2019hawq,dong2020hawqv2} learn per-layer bit-widths via RL or Hessian analysis. We provide the first systematic quantification for \bitnetb{}: \texttt{conv1} accounts for 54--74\% of accuracy degradation with only 0.08\% of parameters.

\section{Method}

\textbf{BitNet b1.58.} We quantize weights to $\{-1,0,+1\}$ using absmean normalization: $\tilde{W} = \text{RoundClip}(W/\gamma, -1, 1)$ where $\gamma = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i|W_i|$. Activations use 8-bit absmax quantization. The straight-through estimator allows gradient flow. We convert standard architectures from \texttt{timm}~\cite{rw2019timm} by replacing all linear/conv layers with BitNet equivalents.

\textbf{Training.} ResNets: SGD (momentum 0.9), lr=0.1, cosine schedule, 200 epochs, batch size 128. Other architectures require adapted hyperparameters: MobileNetV2/EfficientNet-B0 use lr=0.01, ConvNeXt uses AdamW lr=0.004.

\textbf{FP32 benefits 1.5--3.7$\times$ more than ternary.} On CIFAR-100, FP32 ResNet-18 gains +3.48 pp from full augmentation while BitNet gains only +0.94 pp---a 3.7$\times$ differential. For ResNet-50/CIFAR-10, full augmentation actually \emph{hurts} the ternary model ($-$0.46\%) while improving FP32 (+1.64\%). This has a practical consequence: spending effort on augmentation is counterproductive for ternary deployment.

\section{What Works: A Practical Recipe}

\subsection{Layer-wise Ablation}

We conduct layer-wise ablation by selectively keeping individual layers in FP32 while quantizing the rest. Table~\ref{tab:ablation_short} shows that keeping only \texttt{conv1} in FP32 recovers 58\% of the gap on CIFAR-10, despite 0.08\% parameter overhead. Later layers provide minimal benefit---keeping the entire final residual block (45\% of parameters) provides no benefit.

\textbf{Why \texttt{conv1}?} The first convolutional layer processes raw pixel intensities, where subtle gradients encode critical visual information. Ternary weights, restricted to $\{-1,0,+1\}$, cannot capture these fine-grained patterns. Deeper layers operate on increasingly abstract, discrete-like features (edges, textures, parts) that tolerate coarse quantization.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}

Standard soft-label KD ($T=4$, $\alpha=0.9$) transfers knowledge from a full-precision teacher via softened outputs. On CIFAR-10, KD alone recovers 36\% of the gap; on CIFAR-100, it recovers 57\%. The benefit increases with task complexity, as soft labels encode richer inter-class relationships ($\binom{C}{2}$ pairwise relationships scale quadratically with classes).

We ablate temperature $T$ and mixing coefficient $\alpha$ to validate our defaults. Table~\ref{tab:kd_ablation} shows that while individual hyperparameters can be tuned (e.g., $T=5$ for CIFAR-10), the default combination performs best overall due to negative interactions when jointly optimized.

We combine both interventions: keeping \texttt{conv1} in FP32 and training with KD. The effects are largely additive: conv1 alone recovers 58\%, KD alone 36\%, and the combination achieves 88\% recovery on CIFAR-10 (vs.~94\% if fully additive). Table~\ref{tab:recipe_short} shows results across three datasets.

\textbf{Standard convolutions work, depthwise do not.} ResNet achieves strong and consistent recovery (80--130\%), with four configurations exceeding FP32. MobileNetV2 and EfficientNet-B0 show much weaker recovery (17--75\%) and exhibit larger baseline gaps (6--14\% vs ResNet's 3.5--5.8\%). Depthwise separable convolutions with few channels per group are particularly vulnerable: each filter has limited capacity to represent diverse spatial patterns with only three values.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Gap scaling.} The baseline gap grows with task complexity: 3.5\% (CIFAR-10, 10 classes), 4.3\% (CIFAR-100, 100 classes), 5.8\% (Tiny-IN, 200 classes). This aligns with information-theoretic predictions: ternary weights carry $\log_2(3) \approx 1.58$ bits, while output entropy $H(Y) = \log_2(C)$ grows logarithmically with classes. As the classification task demands more information capacity, the ternary network operates closer to its fundamental limit.

\textbf{Why KD helps more on harder tasks.} A one-hot label carries $\log_2(C)$ bits. A soft label encodes inter-class similarity structure---the number of pairwise relationships scales as $\binom{C}{2}$. For CIFAR-10, this is 45 pairs; for CIFAR-100, it is 4,950 pairs---a 110$\times$ increase in relational information. This explains why KD recovers 36\% on CIFAR-10 but 57\% on CIFAR-100.

\textbf{Limitations.} (1) Training recipe not optimized for ImageNet scale. (2) Recipe requires per-architecture hyperparameter tuning. (3) Statistical power limited with $n=3$ seeds; ``exceeds FP32'' claims are individually significant but do not survive strict Bonferroni correction across all 15 configurations ($\alpha_{\text{adj}}=0.003$). They do survive when restricted to the two ``exceeds FP32'' hypotheses ($\alpha_{\text{adj}}=0.025$). (4) No real latency measurements.

\section{Conclusion}

For ternary CNNs: \textbf{invest in distillation, not augmentation}. Data augmentation widens the accuracy gap (up to 59\%), while keeping conv1 in FP32 + knowledge distillation closes it (89\% recovery) and can exceed FP32 on harder tasks (+1.0\% CIFAR-100, +1.7\% Tiny-IN). Architecture choice is critical: standard convolutions (ResNet) achieve 80--130\% recovery, depthwise separable designs (MobileNetV2) only 17--73\%. Practitioners should factor quantization friendliness into architecture selection for edge deployment.
